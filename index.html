3D Math, from algebra to radial basis functions

A short illustrated guide.

Each chapter builds on the previous one. Read in order.

Chapter 1The number line

Before we touch vectors, let us agree on the simplest setting: numbers on a line.

Every real number has a place on the line. Positive numbers to the right, negatives to the left, zero in the middle. The distance between two numbers \(a\) and \(b\) is the absolute value of their difference:

$$ d(a, b) \;=\; |\,a - b\,| $$

The bars mean “always positive.” Distance never depends on which direction you face.

Example

\(a = -2,\;\; b = 3\). Then \(d(a,b) = |-2 - 3| = |-5| = 5\). Swapping them gives \(|3 - (-2)| = |5| = 5\). Same answer.

Key idea Distance is symmetric and never negative. Subtract, then take absolute value.

Chapter 2Vectors in 2D

A vector is two numbers grouped together. Drawn as an arrow from the origin.

A vector in 2D is written

$$ \mathbf{v} = (x, y) $$

where \(x\) and \(y\) are called the components. We draw it as an arrow that starts at the origin \((0, 0)\) and ends at the point \((x, y)\).

The length of \(\mathbf{v}\) (also called magnitude or norm, written \(\|\mathbf{v}\|\)) comes from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the right triangle made by the components:

$$ \|\mathbf{v}\| \;=\; \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} $$
Example

If \(\mathbf{v} = (3, 4)\), then \(\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\). If \(\mathbf{u} = (-2, 0)\), then \(\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{4 + 0} = 2\). Length is never negative.

Two ways to read a vector A position (the point at the tip), or an arrow (a direction with a length). For 3D math we mostly use the arrow view.

Chapter 3Adding and scaling vectors

Add component-wise. Scale by a single number.

Addition

To add two vectors, add their components:

$$ \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} \;=\; (a_x + b_x,\; a_y + b_y) $$

Geometrically, there are two equivalent pictures.

  • Tip to tail: place the tail of \(\mathbf{b}\) at the tip of \(\mathbf{a}\). The arrow from the origin to the new tip is \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\).
  • Parallelogram: draw \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) from the origin. Complete the parallelogram. The diagonal from the origin is \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\).

Subtraction

Subtraction also goes component-wise:

$$ \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b} \;=\; (a_x - b_x,\; a_y - b_y) $$

The geometric meaning matters: \(\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}\) is the displacement that takes you from the tip of \(\mathbf{b}\) to the tip of \(\mathbf{a}\). Its length is the straight-line distance between those two points. Hold on to this — it returns in Chapter 9.

Scalar multiplication

A scalar is a plain number. Multiplying a vector by a scalar \(k\) stretches every component:

$$ k\,\mathbf{a} \;=\; (k\,a_x,\; k\,a_y) $$

Effects:

  • \(k > 1\): the vector gets longer in the same direction.
  • \(0 < k < 1\): it gets shorter.
  • \(k < 0\): it flips to the opposite direction.
  • \(k = -1\): same length, opposite direction.
Example

\(\mathbf{a} = (2, 3)\). Then \(2\mathbf{a} = (4, 6)\) and \(-\mathbf{a} = (-2, -3)\). Both have a clear geometric story: stretch and flip.

Chapter 4Vectors in 3D

Add a third number. The rules do not change.

A 3D vector has three components:

$$ \mathbf{v} = (x, y, z), \qquad \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} $$

Addition, subtraction, scaling: still component-wise.

$$ \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (a_x+b_x,\;a_y+b_y,\;a_z+b_z), \qquad k\,\mathbf{a} = (k a_x,\; k a_y,\; k a_z) $$

The Pythagorean idea generalizes: drop a vertical line from the tip to the \(xy\)-plane to form a right triangle. The length of the projection on the \(xy\)-plane is \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). Combine with the \(z\) leg:

$$ \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 \;=\; \big(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\big)^2 + z^2 \;=\; x^2 + y^2 + z^2 $$
Key idea Everything you learned for 2D carries over to 3D: just include the \(z\) component everywhere. The same is true for 4D, 5D, and so on.

Chapter 5The dot product

A single number that measures how aligned two vectors are.

Definition

The dot product of two vectors is

$$ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} \;=\; a_x b_x + a_y b_y + a_z b_z $$

(For 2D vectors, drop the \(z\) term.) The result is one number, never a vector.

Geometric meaning

There is a second formula, which looks different but always gives the same value:

$$ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} \;=\; \|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta $$

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors. The two formulas together let you compute the angle:

$$ \cos\theta \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|} $$

Because \(\cos\theta\) ranges from \(-1\) to \(+1\), the sign of the dot product tells you the rough direction:

  • Positive: the vectors point in roughly the same direction (acute angle).
  • Zero: the vectors are perpendicular.
  • Negative: they point in roughly opposite directions (obtuse angle).

Projection

The dot product also gives the length of the “shadow” that \(\mathbf{b}\) casts on \(\mathbf{a}\). The projection length is

$$ \text{proj length} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|} $$
Example

Take \(\mathbf{a} = (1, 2, 0)\) and \(\mathbf{b} = (3, 0, 4)\).

\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 1\cdot3 + 2\cdot0 + 0\cdot4 = 3\). Lengths: \(\|\mathbf{a}\| = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.236\), \(\|\mathbf{b}\| = \sqrt{25} = 5\). So \(\cos\theta = 3 / (5\sqrt{5}) \approx 0.268\), giving \(\theta \approx 74.5^\circ\).

Useful identity \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} = \|\mathbf{a}\|^2\). Dotting a vector with itself gives the square of its length.

Chapter 6The cross product

A 3D-only operation that produces a vector perpendicular to the two inputs.

Definition

For \(\mathbf{a} = (a_x, a_y, a_z)\) and \(\mathbf{b} = (b_x, b_y, b_z)\),

$$ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} \;=\; \big(\, a_y b_z - a_z b_y,\;\; a_z b_x - a_x b_z,\;\; a_x b_y - a_y b_x \,\big) $$

Unlike the dot product, the result is a vector, not a number.

Three things to remember

  1. Perpendicularity. \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}\) is perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\). It points out of the plane that they span.
  2. Length equals area. \(\|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}\| = \|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|\sin\theta\), which is exactly the area of the parallelogram with sides \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\).
  3. Right-hand rule. Point your right hand from \(\mathbf{a}\) toward \(\mathbf{b}\). Your thumb points in the direction of \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}\).
Example

\(\mathbf{a} = (1, 0, 0)\) (along x) and \(\mathbf{b} = (0, 1, 0)\) (along y). Plug in:

$$ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = (0\cdot 0 - 0\cdot 1,\;\; 0\cdot 0 - 1\cdot 0,\;\; 1\cdot 1 - 0\cdot 0) = (0, 0, 1) $$

The result points along \(+z\), which is exactly “up” out of the \(xy\)-plane. Swap them: \(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a} = (0, 0, -1)\). Order matters.

Key facts
  • \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = -(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a})\). The cross product is anti-commutative.
  • If \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are parallel, \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}\). They span no plane, so there is no perpendicular direction.
  • The dot product gives an angle (alignment). The cross product gives a perpendicular vector and an area.

Chapter 7Matrices as transformations

A matrix takes every vector and maps it to a new vector. Think of it as a function.

Notation

A 2×2 matrix and its action on a 2D vector:

$$ M \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}, \qquad M\,\mathbf{v} \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} a x + b y \\ c x + d y \end{bmatrix} $$

Read it like a recipe: the first output number is “\(a\) times \(x\), plus \(b\) times \(y\).” The second is “\(c\) times \(x\), plus \(d\) times \(y\).”

The columns are images of the basis

Try plugging in \(\mathbf{v} = (1, 0)\). You get \((a, c)\) — the first column of \(M\). Now try \(\mathbf{v} = (0, 1)\). You get \((b, d)\) — the second column.

This is the most useful way to read a matrix:

Key idea The columns of a matrix tell you where the basis vectors land. \((1,0) \mapsto\) first column, \((0,1) \mapsto\) second column. Every other vector is a combination, so the columns determine the whole transformation.

Common 2×2 transformations

The determinant

For a 2×2 matrix, the determinant is

$$ \det(M) \;=\; ad - bc $$

It is the factor by which the matrix scales area. The unit square has area 1; the parallelogram it gets mapped to has area \(|\det(M)|\). If the determinant is negative, the matrix flips orientation (mirror image).

Chapter 8Matrix multiplication

If you do one transformation then another, that combined operation is also a matrix.

Given two 2×2 matrices \(A\) and \(B\), their product \(AB\) is the matrix that does “first \(B\), then \(A\)”. That is,

$$ (AB)\,\mathbf{v} \;=\; A\,(B\,\mathbf{v}) $$

The formula for the product:

$$ A B \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{bmatrix} \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} ae + bg & af + bh \\ ce + dg & cf + dh \end{bmatrix} $$

Each entry of \(AB\) is a dot product: row \(i\) of \(A\) dotted with column \(j\) of \(B\).

Order matters

In general, \(AB \neq BA\). Rotating then scaling can be different from scaling then rotating, depending on what kind of scaling. Always pay attention to the order.

Example

Let \(A = \big[\begin{smallmatrix}0 & -1\\ 1 & 0\end{smallmatrix}\big]\) (rotate 90°) and \(B = \big[\begin{smallmatrix}2 & 0\\ 0 & 2\end{smallmatrix}\big]\) (scale by 2). Then

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix}0 & -1\\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix}2 & 0\\ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & -2\\ 2 & 0\end{bmatrix} $$

This matrix scales by 2 and rotates by 90°, in one step.

Identity and inverse

The identity matrix does nothing:

$$ I \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad I\,\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}, \qquad I M = M I = M $$

The inverse \(M^{-1}\), when it exists, undoes \(M\):

$$ M\,M^{-1} \;=\; M^{-1} M \;=\; I $$

Multiplying both sides of \(M\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}\) by \(M^{-1}\) gives \(\mathbf{x} = M^{-1}\mathbf{y}\). That is how we “solve” matrix equations. We need this in Chapters 11, 12, and 14.

An inverse exists only when \(\det(M) \neq 0\) — in plain words, when the transformation does not collapse area to zero.

Chapter 9Distance between points

The bridge from algebra to radial basis functions.

Given two points \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{c}\) (in 2D, 3D, or any dimension), the displacement is the vector \(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}\). Its length is the straight-line distance between them:

$$ r \;=\; \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}\| \;=\; \sqrt{(x_1 - c_1)^2 + (x_2 - c_2)^2 + \dots} $$

This is the same Pythagorean formula from Chapter 2, just written with the difference of two points instead of one vector.

Level sets

Now fix a center \(\mathbf{c}\) and treat \(r\) as a function of \(\mathbf{x}\). The set of points at the same distance \(r\) from \(\mathbf{c}\) forms a circle in 2D (a sphere in 3D). These are called level sets.

The level sets are circles because the distance function only depends on \(r\), not on direction. This is what people mean when they say a function is radial.

Key idea Distance from a fixed center is a single number that depends only on how far away you are, not on which way you face. That radial structure is the entry point for the next chapter.

Chapter 10Interpolation

Given a few data points, find a curve that passes through every one. Two natural attempts and one big problem.

Suppose you have \(n+1\) data points

$$ (x_0, y_0),\;(x_1, y_1),\;\ldots,\;(x_n, y_n) $$

sorted so that \(x_0 < x_1 < \dots < x_n\). You want a function \(f(x)\) with \(f(x_i) = y_i\) for every \(i\). This is the interpolation problem in 1D.

Linear interpolation

The simplest answer: connect each pair of neighbouring points with a straight line. On the interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\) the formula is

$$ f(x) \;=\; y_i + (y_{i+1} - y_i)\,\frac{x - x_i}{x_{i+1} - x_i} $$

This is fast and robust. But the curve has a corner at every data point — it is continuous but not smooth.

Polynomial interpolation

A second answer: fit one polynomial of degree \(n\) through all \(n+1\) points. There is exactly one such polynomial, and you can write it down using the Lagrange formula:

$$ p(x) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{n} y_i \prod_{j\neq i} \frac{x - x_j}{x_i - x_j} $$

For a few points this gives a smooth curve. For many points it falls apart.

Force a single polynomial through 11 evenly spaced samples of a smooth function and the polynomial oscillates wildly near the endpoints — its swings get bigger than the data itself. This is the Runge phenomenon, named after the function \(f(x) = 1 / (1 + 25 x^2)\) on which it shows up dramatically.

Key idea Single high-degree polynomials are unstable. Connecting points with straight lines is too rough. The fix: use many low-degree polynomials, glued together smoothly. That is the spline idea.

Chapter 11Cubic splines

A different cubic on each interval, joined at the data points so that values, slopes, and curvatures all agree. The conditions reduce to a tridiagonal linear system.

Setup

Same data points: \((x_0, y_0), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)\). On each interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\) we use a cubic polynomial \(S_i(x)\). That is \(n\) cubics, each with 4 coefficients, so \(4n\) unknowns in total.

Conditions

To pin all \(4n\) unknowns we impose:

  • Interpolation. Each cubic hits the right values at the two ends of its interval: \(S_i(x_i) = y_i\) and \(S_i(x_{i+1}) = y_{i+1}\). That is \(2n\) equations.
  • Slope continuity. At each interior knot \(x_i\) (\(i = 1, \ldots, n-1\)), the cubic on the left and the cubic on the right have the same first derivative: \(S_{i-1}'(x_i) = S_i'(x_i)\). That is \(n-1\) equations.
  • Curvature continuity. Same for second derivatives: \(S_{i-1}''(x_i) = S_i''(x_i)\). Another \(n-1\) equations.

Total so far: \(2n + 2(n-1) = 4n - 2\) equations. Two more are needed. The most common choice is the natural spline:

$$ S''(x_0) = 0, \qquad S''(x_n) = 0 $$

The curve straightens out at the endpoints. (Other choices: clamped fixes the slopes at the ends; periodic matches both sides for closed curves.)

Reducing to a tridiagonal system

Trying to solve for all \(4n\) coefficients directly is messy. The standard trick: introduce

$$ M_i \;=\; S''(x_i), \qquad i = 0, 1, \ldots, n $$

the second derivative at each data point. After some algebra (write each cubic in terms of \(M_i\) and \(M_{i+1}\), then enforce slope continuity at the interior knots) the conditions reduce to a linear system in just the \(M_i\). For equally spaced data with \(h = x_{i+1} - x_i\),

$$ M_{i-1} \,+\, 4\,M_i \,+\, M_{i+1} \;=\; \frac{6}{h^2}\,(y_{i+1} - 2 y_i + y_{i-1}), \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n-1 $$

Each equation links three neighbouring \(M\)'s, no further. With natural boundary conditions \(M_0 = M_n = 0\), the unknowns are \(M_1, \ldots, M_{n-1}\), and the matrix has only three non-zero diagonals — it is tridiagonal:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 & & & \\ 1 & 4 & 1 & & \\ & 1 & 4 & \ddots & \\ & & \ddots & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} M_1 \\ M_2 \\ \vdots \\ M_{n-1} \end{bmatrix} \;=\; \frac{6}{h^2}\begin{bmatrix} y_2 - 2 y_1 + y_0 \\ y_3 - 2 y_2 + y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_n - 2 y_{n-1} + y_{n-2} \end{bmatrix} $$

Tridiagonal systems are solved by the Thomas algorithm — a one-sweep version of Gaussian elimination that takes only \(O(n)\) time. Once you have all \(M_i\), the cubic on each interval is

$$ S_i(x) = \tfrac{M_i}{6h}(x_{i+1}-x)^3 + \tfrac{M_{i+1}}{6h}(x-x_i)^3 + \!\left(\tfrac{y_i}{h} - \tfrac{M_i h}{6}\right)\!(x_{i+1}-x) + \!\left(\tfrac{y_{i+1}}{h} - \tfrac{M_{i+1} h}{6}\right)\!(x-x_i) $$

Worked example: 4 points

Take the data \((0,0), (1,1), (2,0), (3,1)\). Spacing \(h = 1\). Natural spline: \(M_0 = M_3 = 0\), and we solve for \(M_1, M_2\).

The right-hand sides:

$$ \frac{6}{1^2}\,(y_2 - 2 y_1 + y_0) = 6\,(0 - 2 + 0) = -12, \qquad \frac{6}{1^2}\,(y_3 - 2 y_2 + y_1) = 6\,(1 - 0 + 1) = 12 $$

So the 2×2 system is

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} M_1 \\ M_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -12 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix} $$

From row 1: \(M_2 = -12 - 4 M_1\). Substitute into row 2: \(M_1 + 4(-12 - 4 M_1) = 12 \Rightarrow -15 M_1 = 60 \Rightarrow M_1 = -4\). Then \(M_2 = -12 - 4(-4) = 4\).

So \(M = (0, -4, 4, 0)\). The three cubics are now fully determined. Plug into the formula above to get, for example, on the first interval \([0, 1]\):

$$ S_0(x) \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{3} x^3 + \tfrac{5}{3} x $$

You can check directly: \(S_0(0) = 0\), \(S_0(1) = 1\), \(S_0''(0) = 0\), \(S_0''(1) = -4\). All conditions satisfied. The full spline curve:

The matrix payoff Splines reduce a continuous-curve problem to \(A\,\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{y}\), exactly the kind of system Chapter 8 introduced. For splines, \(A\) has only three non-zero diagonals, so the system is fast to solve. In Chapters 13 and 14, RBF interpolation will follow the same pattern in higher dimensions, but with a dense \(A\) whose entries are kernel values.

Chapter 12Barycentric coordinates

Three points define a triangle. Any other point can be written as a weighted average of the three. Those weights are the point's barycentric coordinates.

Definition

Take three points \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) in the plane (not collinear, so they form a real triangle). Pick any point \(P\) in the plane. Then there exist three numbers \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) such that

$$ P \;=\; \alpha\,A + \beta\,B + \gamma\,C, \qquad \alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1 $$

The triple \((\alpha, \beta, \gamma)\) is the barycentric coordinates of \(P\) with respect to the triangle \(ABC\). They tell you how much of each vertex goes into making \(P\).

Special points

  • \((1, 0, 0)\) is just \(A\). Similarly \((0, 1, 0) = B\) and \((0, 0, 1) = C\).
  • \(\big(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}\big)\) is the centroid — the average of the three vertices, which is the triangle's centre of mass.
  • If one coordinate is zero, \(P\) lies on the opposite edge. For example, \(\gamma = 0\) means \(P\) is on the line \(AB\).
  • If all three coordinates are positive, \(P\) is strictly inside the triangle. If any is negative, \(P\) lies outside.

The area interpretation

There is a beautiful geometric meaning. If \(P\) is inside the triangle, draw the three line segments from \(P\) to the vertices. This splits \(ABC\) into three sub-triangles \(PBC\), \(PCA\), \(PAB\). Then

$$ \alpha = \frac{\text{Area}(PBC)}{\text{Area}(ABC)}, \quad \beta = \frac{\text{Area}(PCA)}{\text{Area}(ABC)}, \quad \gamma = \frac{\text{Area}(PAB)}{\text{Area}(ABC)} $$

The coordinate \(\alpha\) (the weight of \(A\)) is the relative area of the sub-triangle opposite \(A\). The three sub-areas always add up to the total — that is exactly why \(\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1\). Because of this geometric meaning, barycentric coordinates are also called areal coordinates.

From Chapter 6 you know that the area of a triangle in 2D is half the magnitude of a cross product:

$$ \text{Area}(ABC) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\big|(B - A)\times(C - A)\big| $$

so areas (and therefore barycentric coordinates) are quick to compute from vector algebra alone.

Computing the coordinates

Given a point \(P\), you can find its barycentric coordinates by solving a small linear system. Use \(\gamma = 1 - \alpha - \beta\) to eliminate \(\gamma\):

$$ P - C \;=\; \alpha\,(A - C) + \beta\,(B - C) $$

This is two scalar equations (one per coordinate of \(P\)) in two unknowns \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) — exactly the kind of 2×2 system you solved in Chapter 8.

Worked example

Take \(A = (0, 0)\), \(B = (4, 0)\), \(C = (2, 3)\), and \(P = (2, 1)\).

Compute the differences:

$$ A - C = (-2, -3), \quad B - C = (2, -3), \quad P - C = (0, -2) $$

The 2×2 system \(P - C = \alpha(A-C) + \beta(B-C)\) is

$$ \begin{aligned} -2\alpha + 2\beta &= 0 \\ -3\alpha - 3\beta &= -2 \end{aligned} $$

From the first equation, \(\alpha = \beta\). Substitute into the second: \(-3\alpha - 3\alpha = -2\), so \(\alpha = \beta = \tfrac{1}{3}\). Then \(\gamma = 1 - \alpha - \beta = \tfrac{1}{3}\).

Check: \(\tfrac{1}{3}(0,0) + \tfrac{1}{3}(4,0) + \tfrac{1}{3}(2,3) = (2, 1) = P\). ✓ So \(P\) is the centroid, as expected from its symmetric position.

Linear interpolation on a triangle

Once you have barycentric coordinates, you have a free interpolation method. Given values \(f_A, f_B, f_C\) at the three vertices, define

$$ f(P) \;=\; \alpha\,f_A + \beta\,f_B + \gamma\,f_C $$

This is the unique linear function that takes the right value at all three vertices. Inside the triangle it varies smoothly and linearly. It is the 2D analogue of the linear interpolation from Chapter 10.

Where this is used
  • Computer graphics: rendering triangles uses barycentric coordinates for textures, shading, and ray–triangle intersection tests.
  • The finite element method uses them to define piecewise-linear basis functions on a triangulated domain.
  • Mesh-based interpolation: if your data lives on a triangle mesh, barycentric coordinates blend the vertex values.
All of this needs a mesh. The next chapters tackle the case where you only have scattered points and no mesh — that is what radial basis functions are for.

Chapter 13Radial basis functions

Apply any function to the distance \(r\). You get a function whose value depends only on how far you are from a center.

A radial basis function (RBF) is a function \(\phi(r)\) of the single variable \(r\). Given a center \(\mathbf{c}\), the “basis function centered at \(\mathbf{c}\)” is

$$ \phi\big(\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}\|\big) $$

Two points equally far from \(\mathbf{c}\) get the same value, no matter where they are around \(\mathbf{c}\). That is what “radial” means.

The four classics

These are the kernels you will see most often. \(\varepsilon\) is a shape parameter (called the shape or scale): bigger \(\varepsilon\) means narrower bumps.

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Gaussian:} && \phi(r) &= e^{-(\varepsilon r)^2} \\ \text{Multiquadric:} && \phi(r) &= \sqrt{1 + (\varepsilon r)^2} \\ \text{Inverse multiquadric:} && \phi(r) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\varepsilon r)^2}} \\ \text{Thin-plate spline:} && \phi(r) &= r^2 \log r \quad (\phi(0)=0) \end{aligned} $$

What a 2D radial bump looks like

Take the Gaussian. Centered at \(\mathbf{c} = (0, 0)\), the value at any point \(\mathbf{x} = (x, y)\) is

$$ \phi\big(\|\mathbf{x}\|\big) \;=\; e^{-\varepsilon^2(x^2 + y^2)} $$

This is a smooth bump, tallest at the center, decaying outward. The level sets are circles — the same circles from Chapter 9.

Local versus global Gaussian and inverse multiquadric are local: they decay to zero as \(r\) grows. Multiquadric and thin-plate are global: they keep growing. Both kinds are useful for different jobs.

Chapter 14RBF interpolation

Place several bumps, each at a known data point. Choose their weights so that the sum passes exactly through every point. This is where vectors, distances, and matrices all meet.

Setup

Suppose you have \(n\) data points

$$ (\mathbf{x}_1, y_1),\; (\mathbf{x}_2, y_2),\; \ldots,\; (\mathbf{x}_n, y_n) $$

where each \(\mathbf{x}_i\) is a position (in 1D, 2D, 3D — pick your dimension) and each \(y_i\) is a value at that position (a height, a temperature, a density). You want a smooth function \(f(\mathbf{x})\) that equals \(y_i\) at every \(\mathbf{x}_i\).

The RBF idea

Build \(f\) as a sum of \(n\) bumps, one centered at each data point, each with its own weight \(w_i\):

$$ f(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i\,\phi\!\big(\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_i\|\big) $$

The matrix system

To find the weights, demand that \(f(\mathbf{x}_j) = y_j\) at every data point. That is one equation per \(j\):

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i\,\phi\!\big(\|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|\big) \;=\; y_j \qquad (j = 1, 2, \dots, n) $$

That is exactly an n × n linear system in the unknowns \(w_1, \ldots, w_n\). Stack the equations in matrix form:

$$ A\,\mathbf{w} \;=\; \mathbf{y}, \qquad A_{ij} \;=\; \phi\!\big(\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|\big) $$

Each entry of \(A\) is the kernel evaluated at the distance between two data points. The diagonal entries are \(\phi(0)\), because every point is at distance zero from itself.

Now use Chapter 8: solve for \(\mathbf{w}\) by multiplying both sides by \(A^{-1}\):

$$ \mathbf{w} \;=\; A^{-1}\mathbf{y} $$

Once you have \(\mathbf{w}\), the function \(f(\mathbf{x})\) is fully defined — you can evaluate it anywhere.

Why this works \(n\) unknowns, \(n\) equations. The matrix \(A\) is symmetric (because distance is symmetric: \(\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\| = \|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\|\)) and, for the Gaussian and IMQ, invertible. The interpolant exists and is unique.

Worked example: 3 points in 1D, Gaussian kernel

Take the data points \((0, 1)\), \((1, 0)\), \((2, 1)\). Use the Gaussian kernel with \(\varepsilon = 1\). So \(\phi(r) = e^{-r^2}\). The pairwise distances:

$$ \begin{array}{c|ccc} & x_1=0 & x_2=1 & x_3=2 \\ \hline x_1=0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \\ x_2=1 & 1 & 0 & 1 \\ x_3=2 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ \end{array} $$

Apply \(\phi(r) = e^{-r^2}\) to each entry to build \(A\):

$$ A \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} e^0 & e^{-1} & e^{-4} \\ e^{-1} & e^0 & e^{-1} \\ e^{-4} & e^{-1} & e^0 \end{bmatrix} \;\approx\; \begin{bmatrix} 1.000 & 0.368 & 0.018 \\ 0.368 & 1.000 & 0.368 \\ 0.018 & 0.368 & 1.000 \end{bmatrix} $$

And \(\mathbf{y} = (1, 0, 1)\). Solve \(A\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{y}\). By symmetry of the data, \(w_1 = w_3\). Setting \(w_1 = w_3 = a\) and \(w_2 = b\) and solving the small system gives

$$ \mathbf{w} \;\approx\; (1.338,\; -0.984,\; 1.338) $$

Therefore

$$ f(x) \;=\; 1.338\,e^{-x^2} \;-\; 0.984\,e^{-(x-1)^2} \;+\; 1.338\,e^{-(x-2)^2} $$

Check at the data points:

  • \(f(0) \approx 1.338 - 0.984\cdot e^{-1} + 1.338\cdot e^{-4} \approx 1.338 - 0.362 + 0.024 = 1.000\) ✓
  • \(f(1) \approx 1.338\cdot e^{-1} - 0.984 + 1.338\cdot e^{-1} \approx 0.984 - 0.984 = 0\) ✓
  • \(f(2) \approx 1.000\) by symmetry ✓

What you have learned

An RBF interpolant is built from three ingredients you now know:

  • Vectors and distance — the argument of \(\phi\).
  • A function \(\phi\) of distance — the “radial” piece.
  • A linear system \(A\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{y}\) — the matrix piece, solved by inversion.

That is the whole idea. Nothing more is needed to write down an RBF interpolant in any number of dimensions. The same method underlies smooth scattered-data interpolation in graphics, geostatistics (where it is called kriging), neural networks (RBF networks), and meshless methods for solving differential equations.

End of guide. You have gone from \(|a-b|\) on a line to fitting a smooth surface through scattered points using a linear system whose entries are functions of vector distances.
